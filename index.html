Quick Start — The Five Steps
1
Sign in
Relai sends you a one-time sign-in link by email (a "magic link"). No password. Click the link from your email and you're in. Your admin creates your account before your first sign-in — if you've never received an invite, ask them.
2
Paste the patient message
Drop the patient's current message into the left box on the Triage tab. If it's a reply to an ongoing conversation, click Add Prior Context and add each earlier turn — pick the speaker, type what they said, click + Add turn for the next one. Oldest at top, newest reply at the bottom.
3
Click Analyze
The AI reads the message against your Knowledge Base and returns a classification, routing decision, and a draft response (for clinical staff) or a handoff template (for non-clinical staff handling clinical content). Usually takes 5–15 seconds.
4
Check the results top to bottom
Classification card — urgency and category. Routing card — appears when part of the message also needs another team; share the internal note with them through your usual handoff method. Generated Response — copy and send to patient.
5
Teach the AI if you edited
If you changed the draft before sending, paste what you actually sent into What was sent to the patient and click Submit & Learn. Only do this when you made changes. Sending the draft verbatim is already a positive signal — don't paste it back.
First-Day Walkthrough
You'll land on the Triage tab. Top right is your profile chip with your initial and your role (RN for clinical, CS for non-clinical). Click the chip to open your profile drawer — that's where the AI Review Worklist, Inquiry History, Help & Guide, and Sign Out live.

For your first triage, pick a recent patient message from your usual channel (email, chat, whatever you normally use), paste it into the message box, and click Analyze. Read the result top to bottom. The first one or two will feel slower than typing a reply yourself; the time is in reading the AI's draft and judging whether to send it as-is or edit. After ten or so messages it'll feel quicker than typing from scratch.

Don't worry about the buttons you don't recognize yet — the rest of this guide covers them. Worth knowing today: the Knowledge Base tab is where the AI's policies and protocols live, and any edits you make there take effect on the very next message.
Reading the Results
The top-left card shows the AI's task type and the severity badge (when a side effect is flagged). The top-right card holds the category pills you can correct. Check both before sending — if a category is wrong, click the correct pill and Save.
Side Effect: Severe means escalate to a provider immediately. Side Effect: Moderate means gather more information and reassess. Side Effect: Mild means an education response is appropriate. The badge only appears when a side effect is detected.
Appears when a message has both clinical and non-clinical parts. The Internal Note for Staff describes what the support team needs to do (ship a replacement, process a refund, transfer a pharmacy, etc.). You — whoever ran this triage — stay responsible for the patient reply. Share the internal note with the support team through whatever handoff method your team uses (a thread comment, an internal email, a ticket); don't include it in the message you send the patient.
Routine means respond in normal queue order. Same Day means the patient needs a reply today. URGENT means escalate now. If wrong, adjust the dropdown and click the green checkmark to save.
Clinical: Side Effect, Medication Question, Symptom Report, Dosing, Lab/Result Question, and similar. Non-clinical: Billing, Shipping, Account, Appointment, and General Inquiry. The exact list is tenant-configurable — an admin can mark categories as clinical-only or non-clinical-only from the Admin tab. Non-clinical staff can only pick from the non-clinical list when correcting.
Using Prior Context
Any time the patient message is a reply to an ongoing conversation. Without the earlier turns the AI is triaging a fragment, and may re-explain things the patient already knows or re-ask questions they already answered. Click Add Prior Context to open the turn list. Each row has a speaker dropdown (Patient / Nurse / Other) and a text field; click + Add turn for each new turn. Put the oldest turn at the top and the most recent reply at the bottom. Then type the newest patient message in the main box below.
No. For a patient's first message — one that isn't a reply to anything — leave Prior Context empty.
AI Review Worklist
When the AI is uncertain about something — an unclear category, a routing question, low confidence on its own draft — it flags the message for a clinician to weigh in. Those flagged items collect in the worklist. The badge on the profile button shows the count. Clear the worklist as part of your normal workflow; the resolutions feed back into the Knowledge Base.
Open the worklist, read the AI's question and the original patient message, then type the answer. Choose what kind of resolution it is: a one-off correction (just this case), a KB gap (this needs to become part of the standing knowledge base), or a protocol (a new if/then rule). KB-gap and protocol resolutions auto-promote into the Knowledge Base — the next time a similar message comes in, the AI already knows.
The item disappears from the worklist and the badge count drops. If you marked it as a KB gap or a protocol, a new entry appears in the Knowledge Base under the appropriate section. You can edit or delete that entry later from the KB tab if you change your mind.
Knowledge Base
Everything the AI reads before responding to a patient message — protocols, side effect guidance, routing rules, urgency thresholds, and response templates. Any change takes effect immediately on the next triage.
Side Effects — education and management guidance.
Templates — response frameworks for common messages.
Protocols — if/then decision logic.
Rules & Notes — urgency thresholds, severity rules. Edit carefully.
Routing — when and how to escalate or route.
URLs — links you reference in patient responses.
Open the Knowledge Base tab, pick the section, click + Add. Give it a clear title (the AI sees the title too — "Nausea on semaglutide week 2" beats "Nausea note") and write the body. Save and the next triage uses it. Most KB entries originate from worklist resolutions; manual entries are for things you know the AI will need before the first patient asks.
Specific, short, and unambiguous. The AI reads every entry on every message, so vague guidance ("be careful with patients on insulin") doesn't help — concrete conditions do ("If the patient mentions injecting more than the prescribed dose, escalate to clinical and ask about timing and amount"). Avoid contradicting other entries; if you're updating a rule, edit the existing entry rather than adding a second one.
Glossary
Urgency — Routine / Same Day / URGENT. How fast the patient needs a reply.

Severity — Mild / Moderate / Severe. How serious a flagged side effect is. Only shown when a side effect is detected.

Priority score — A 1–10 number combining severity, clinical content, and urgency. Used to sort the queue. Higher = more pressing.

Confidence gate — The AI's internal threshold for "I'm sure enough to recommend this." Below it, the message goes to the AI Review Worklist instead of straight to a draft response.

Edit distance — Character-level difference between the AI's draft and what you actually sent. Used to compute Edit Rate.

Escalation — Routing a message to clinical when non-clinical staff received it (or when the AI detected clinical content above non-clinical's scope).

KB promotion — When a worklist resolution becomes a permanent Knowledge Base entry, so the AI handles similar messages on its own next time.

Handoff template — The standardized acknowledgment a non-clinical staff member sends the patient when escalating clinical content. Configured by admins.
Teaching the AI
After sending your response, if you changed the draft, paste what you actually sent and click Submit & Learn. The AI compares the two versions and saves a learning note. If you sent the draft verbatim, skip this entirely.
Click the correct category pill in the Classification card (Clinical or Non-Clinical), then click Save. Each correction is stored and builds a dataset that improves the AI's classification accuracy over time.
Go to Knowledge Base → Corrections tab and click Load Response Corrections. Find the incorrect entry and click Delete.
Use them to flag whether the draft was usable as-is. Thumbs up = good response, send the draft. Thumbs down = needs work — followed by a correction. Both feed into the dataset.
Inquiry History & Stats
Open your profile (click the chip in the top-right corner) for your workspace info. From there, AI Review Worklist opens the clarifications the AI flagged and is waiting on a clinical answer for. Inquiry History opens the team-wide view: aggregate stats and a per-staff breakdown (total inquiries, average priority score, escalation rate, edit rate, average time per inquiry), then the full record list. Inquiry History doesn't auto-load — click Load when you want to fetch records.
An inquiry counts as escalated whenever the AI flagged a side effect at any severity level (mild, moderate, or severe), OR when a non-clinical staff member manually escalated it. It's a routing signal, not a staff-only action.
Percent of inquiries where staff actually changed the AI's draft before sending (measured by character-level edit distance). Verbatim sends — where staff copied the AI's draft as-is and clicked Submit & Learn — don't count toward this number; those are positive signal that the AI got it right. A high rate means the AI's draft frequently needs work; that's useful training data for the corrections feed.
The mean of every inquiry's priority score (a 1–10 number combining severity, clinical content, and urgency). It's a rough indicator of the case mix coming through. A rising average means more pressing messages on average — useful for staffing decisions, not for grading individuals.
The mean number of seconds from when an inquiry is opened to when it's sent. It only counts inquiries where the timing was actually recorded (rows where the staff member used the full Triage → Send flow). Rows without a duration are excluded so the average doesn't get dragged down by zeros.
The Message column shows the first ~80 characters of the patient message at a glance — usually enough to identify a row. For full detail, click anywhere on the row (except the × button) to expand it inline: full patient message, AI draft response, what was actually sent (only shown when it differs from the draft), follow-up questions, and any internal note for the support team. Click the same row again to collapse.
Yes. Each row has a small × on the right edge — click it to delete that inquiry permanently. The confirmation dialog quotes the message back to you before anything is destroyed. Three checkpoints stand between you and an accidental delete: the Message column preview, optional click-to-expand, and the confirm dialog itself. KB entries already promoted from a deleted inquiry are not removed — the lesson the AI learned survives. Non-clinical staff can only delete non-clinical-tier rows.
Roles & Escalation
Clinical staff (RNs/MDs) handle anything — clinical questions, non-clinical questions, escalations from non-clinical. Non-clinical staff (CSRs) handle non-clinical inquiries (Billing, Shipping, Account, etc.) and General Inquiry. When they receive clinical content, they hit Escalate to Clinical and the nursing team picks it up. Severity (mild/moderate/severe), clinical category selection, and editing clinical drafts are all clinical-only.
Paste the patient message and click Analyze. If the AI flags clinical content, you'll see a handoff card with the patient message and a generic acknowledgment ("I've passed your message to our nursing team..."). Copy the acknowledgment, send it to the patient through your usual channel, then click Mark as Escalated. The nursing team sees the inquiry in their queue and replies directly.
Non-clinical staff can edit non-clinical categories (Billing, Shipping, etc.) but cannot touch clinical categories or severity. Severity isn't editable from the UI by anyone — it's set by the AI. If non-clinical sees clinical content the AI missed, they hit Escalate to Clinical rather than picking a clinical category themselves.
Admin Tasks
Open the Admin tab from your profile drawer. In the Users section, click + Invite, enter the new user's email, and pick their role (Clinical or Non-Clinical). They'll receive a magic-link email and become active on first sign-in. You stay scoped to your own tenant — you can't invite someone into another workspace.
In the Admin tab's Users list, each row has the user's current role and admin flag. Change either and the update saves immediately. Setting admin on someone gives them access to the Admin tab; super-user (a higher tier) unlocks tenant-wide settings like category clinical-gating and the handoff template — only existing super-users can grant super-user.
Super-user only. Each category has an is_clinical flag. Categories flagged as clinical can only be selected (or corrected to) by clinical staff; non-clinical staff see only the non-clinical ones in their dropdown. Use this to tighten which messages each role can fully handle.
Super-user only. The body of the generic acknowledgment a non-clinical staff member sends to the patient when escalating clinical content (e.g. "Thanks for your message — I've passed it to our nursing team and they'll be in touch shortly"). Their personal greeting and signoff are added automatically at send time, so keep this body-only. Save once and every non-clinical escalation uses it until you change it again.
Sign-in & Account
Relai uses magic-link auth — no passwords. Enter your email on the sign-in page, click the link in the email you receive, and you're in. Your session stays valid in this browser until you click Sign Out or the session expires (rare; the app silently refreshes it in the background).
Go back to the sign-in page, enter your email, and request a new magic link. If you never receive it, check your spam folder and confirm your email is exactly what your admin used to invite you. If you're new and have never received an invite, ask your admin to create your account in the Admin tab.
Open your profile (top-right chip) and click Sign Out at the bottom. Always sign out on shared computers — magic-link auth means anyone who can read your inbox could sign back in, but the session is local to the browser otherwise.
When Something Goes Wrong
If the AI's classification or draft is clearly off, do not send it. Edit or rewrite the response, then use Submit & Learn with what you actually sent. If the category is wrong, correct it via the pill. If the AI produced something malformed or unreadable — missing fields, broken text — the system automatically routes the row to human review and logs the failure; you should see a notice on the result card. Never copy a malformed draft to a patient.
Most failures are short network blips. Wait a few seconds and click Analyze again with the same message. If it consistently fails on every message, the AI provider may be down — handle messages manually for now and let your admin know. Your draft work isn't lost: the patient-message box keeps its contents until you clear it.
Common Mistakes
Triaging a reply without Prior Context — the AI gets a fragment and produces a generic response.

Ignoring the red severity badge — Severe means escalate to a provider now.

Ignoring the routing card — if a non-clinical item is flagged, the internal note needs to reach the support team via your usual handoff method. Don't paste it into the patient-facing reply.

Entering the wrong thing in the patient-message field or in "What was sent to the patient" — this pollutes the AI's learning signal. Two ways to fix: delete the whole entry from the Inquiry History list (× on the row) when the entire row is garbage, or open Knowledge Base → Corrections tab when only the correction note is wrong.

Pasting the AI's draft back into "What was sent" when you didn't change it — this looks like a heavy edit to the system and skews Edit Rate down. Only paste back when you actually changed the draft.
